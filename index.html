Example 1

How Much data is collected every minute of the day?

Source: Forbes

What the chart represents?

A graph from a 2017 Forbes article titled “How Much Data Is Collected Every Minute of the Day?” attempts to showcase the intensity of internet usage across the globe. What is interesting about the chart is that it quantifies human interaction with the internet via many social media and utility platforms at a minute resolution. The chart also indicates the growth of the internet population over the years at the bottom.

Idiom Issue:

The choice of chart isn't appropriate for this representation for several reasons:

  1. Misleading Channels: At first glance, the chart seems to have five channels (name, logo, annotations, pie quadrant, and color), but in reality, it only contains two informative channels (Internet applications and value count).
  2. Failure to Convey Data Trends: The chart fails to clearly convey the intensity of the data utilized or the trends in data consumption.

Misleading Elements:

  • Arbitrary use of logos.
  • Irregular font sizes.
  • Colors that don't categorize similar media types.
  • Size of pie quadrants not related to data.
  • High ink-to-information ratio.

Data issue:

  • The value count for all nominal data is unique and non-comparable. For example, how can a user compare the number of Tinder swipes with Uber rides?

Suggestion:

  • For an apple-to-apple comparison, the data used could be standardized to bytes of information transferred each minute.
  • Categorizing apps into common groups such as Entertainment, Social, Productivity, and Finance, etc.
  • Finally, a simple line graph or grouped bar graph representing the trends in internet usage, with internet population on one axis and average data (in units of bytes) per minute on another axis, would be better. Coloring the categories could aid in better readability as well.

Example 2

Carbon emission in London

Source: Praveen Govindarajan

What the chart represents?

A data visualization of London's 2019 carbon emissions from different land uses, self-made for my master's thesis. The Mayor of London provides a dataset pertaining to emissions from various sources such as coal, gas, and other sources for land use types residential, commercial, and industrial for every fixed square kilometer. This visualization overlays all the emissions from various land use types by aggregating the emissions from various sources. The radius of the circle maps the aggregated value of emissions. The aim of this visualization is to present users with information on which part of London emits the most carbon.

Idiom Issue:

The representation is not intuitive, as there is no indication of how to relate the size of the circles to the quantity of emissions. The choice of visualization does not completely aid in objectively answering the question of which part of London emits the most carbon. The legends and grid information are too small for the screen. The base map consists only of the road network of London. The data are visualized based on the coordinates provided in the data directly.

Data issue:

  • The missing data were not treated properly, which misleads that a few pockets of the city are emission-free.

Suggestion:

  • Emissions for each planning boundary could have been estimated to scientifically infer which zone emits the most carbon.
  • A pie chart within each planning boundary indicating which land use category emits the most carbon would have been useful.
  • Disclose missing data.
